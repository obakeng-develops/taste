# Taste references

The feed. Append examples over time — links, screenshots, products, before/afters.
For each, write the **why**: the specific thing that works or fails. Bare links
don't build taste; the articulation does.

Format:
```
## <name / link>
- What: <what it is>
- Why it works / fails: <the specific mechanism — alignment, hierarchy, the arc, restraint…>
- Steal: <the reusable move>
```

---

## Foundational reading
- **PostHog — Good Taste & Great Products** — taste is learnable; master affordances,
  kill jank, prototype before committing, feed your taste constantly.
- **Practical Typography — Typography in Ten Minutes** — body text is 95% of it;
  the five-rule priority order and concrete numbers.
- **Ellen Lupton — Design is Storytelling** — flows as narrative arcs; show don't
  tell; the user completes the story.
- **William Zinsser — On Writing Well** — clear writing is clear thinking; cut
  clutter, plain words, active verbs, one thought per sentence, warm human voice;
  rewriting is the work.

## When a metaphor beats the plain word (prose)
- What: "PostHog's definition is the **load-bearing** one" — a vivid metaphor kept
  where Zinsser's plain-word rule would cut it.
- Why it works: plain words are the default, not a law. A live metaphor that adds
  warmth and voice earns more than the plain word it replaces; sterile-correct prose
  is its own clutter. The test: does it make the reader *feel* the point faster?
- Steal: keep a metaphor when it carries voice and lands the idea quicker; cut it the
  moment it's decoration or strains. Break the rule on purpose, never by reflex.
